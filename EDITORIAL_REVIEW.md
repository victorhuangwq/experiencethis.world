# Editorial Review: The Last Three Posts

**Scope:** "The Back Button That Wasn't There" (BackToOpener draft), "Tambourine Voice," and "We Built a Manga Machine That Actually Works."

**Reference for "your voice":** the Pokemon prerelease post, the Singapore food list, and the ARC review.

**The short version:** your instinct is right. The material in the new posts is good — often better than the old posts — but the *delivery* has drifted from "telling a friend about something I did" to "copywriting for the thing I did." The fix isn't cutting content. It's changing the posture: stop selling, go back to reporting.

---

## Part 1: What changed in the voice

### What your older posts do

Reading the Pokemon, Singapore food, and ARC posts back to back, the voice has a few consistent habits:

1. **Self-deprecation before self-promotion.** The Pokemon post opens: *"In typical developer fashion, I over-engineered the solution by building a small website instead of just using a phone timer and printed rules."* You undercut yourself in sentence one, which buys credibility for everything after.

2. **You report what flopped, unprompted.** *"It worked great on mobile, though we used it less than expected during the event."* *"This was most used by my friends to be honest."* Nobody made you say the timer went unused. That honesty is the single most distinctive thing about your old voice.

3. **Uncertainty stays in.** *"...and a side of salted vegetables and I think youtiao (fried dough fritters) too?"* *"I honestly don't have any other Nasi Lemak place to strongly recommend yet. So I will keep this on the list until I find a better place."* You leave the seams showing. It reads like a person, not a publication.

4. **Titles describe, they don't pitch.** "Singapore Food Recommendations." "Pokemon Prerelease Event built with LLMs." "ARC: my new daily browser." Flat, informative, zero adrenaline.

5. **Endings just... stop.** *"If you're interested, check out the mini-site here (feel free to use it for your own events!)"* One low-key sentence with a parenthetical. No closing argument, no call to action, no thesis restated.

6. **Enthusiasm is personal, not persuasive.** ARC ends: *"Right now, I'm just simply excited about a new way to surf the web."* You're describing your own excitement, not manufacturing the reader's.

### What the new posts do instead

The three recent posts have picked up a set of habits that all come from the same place — marketing copy and LinkedIn-style thought leadership:

1. **Ad headlines.** "We Built a Manga Machine **That Actually Works**." "Open Source Dictation **That Actually Gets Your Words Right**." "That Actually X" is a direct-response copywriting formula. Two posts in a row using the same formula makes it look like a template.

2. **Conversion endings.** All three posts end by closing a sale:
   - *"Want to turn your stories into manga? Check out the Story to Manga Machine at app.storytomanga.com."*
   - *"## Try it out ... And if you write a prompt pack for your field, share it. That's kind of the whole point."*
   - The BackToOpener draft ends on theme-restatement rather than a CTA, but it's the same move: making sure the reader leaves with the message.

3. **Engineered rhythm.** Staccato fragments and one-line paragraphs used for drama:
   - *"You reach for the back button. / It's grey. Disabled."*
   - *"Real users, real behavior, real data."*
   - *"That's non-negotiable."*
   - *"The machine doesn't judge. It just makes manga."*
   
   Your old posts are full of ordinary, slightly run-on sentences. The new ones sound like someone performing at an open mic. One or two fragments per post is seasoning; these posts use it as the base rhythm.

4. **The "Not X. It's Y." pivot.** *"This isn't a Bing problem or a ChatGPT problem. It's a browser problem."* *"The LLM is a formatter, not an assistant."* *"It's not just transcription."* It's a genuinely useful rhetorical move — but it appears many times across the three posts, and each use makes the next one cheaper.

5. **Aphorism packaging.** *"The distance between obvious and done is exactly where product craft lives."* *"The beauty of keeping it simple is that people immediately get it."* Your old posts had takeaways too (the Pokemon post has five), but they were plain observations — *"Initial prompts matter"* — not quotable poster copy.

6. **The reader as a prospect.** *"If you're a doctor, a lawyer, a researcher, or anyone who's been frustrated by dictation tools mangling your terminology, I think this is worth trying."* That's audience segmentation. Your old posts never told the reader who they are.

7. **Only escalation, no deflation.** The old posts admit the timer went unused. In the new posts, every reveal is bigger than the last: *"That one blew my mind."* *"I can't stop using it."* *"wait, this might actually work."* When everything is amazing, the reader stops trusting any of it. (Notably, the best moments in the new posts are the deflationary ones you did keep — the music tradeoff in Tambourine, "I'm not counting chickens" in BackToOpener.)

One more honest observation: these patterns — fragment rhythm, "Not X. It's Y.", em-dash density, aphorism closers — are also the tics of AI-assisted drafting. Whether or not that's how these drafts happened, readers increasingly *recognize* this register, and it reads as generic even when the underlying material is deeply personal. The de-salesing pass and the de-genericizing pass are the same pass.

## Part 2: Post-by-post

## A. "We Built a Manga Machine That Actually Works" (2025-09-10)

### Overall read

The strongest raw material here is the *hackathon story*: the timezone relay, the Japanese-speech-bubbles bug, the meta sample story about yourselves. That's the stuff only you can write. The salesy tone concentrates in three places: the title, the "features we knew people would want" list, and the entire final third ("What's next" through the end), which reads like a launch announcement rather than a debrief.

There's also a verbal tic specific to this post: the rhetorical-question-then-answer pattern. *"but honestly? They kind of sucked."* *"But the funniest bug?"* *"My favorite touch?"* *"But from the user's perspective? They just see magic."* *"And honestly? It just works."* Five instances in a short post. Each one is a little drumroll, and by the third the reader hears the formula.

### Structural recommendation

Reframe the post from **launch announcement** to **hackathon debrief**. Concretely:

- Keep: the opening context, the timezone relay, the technical quirks section, the pipeline explanation, the meta sample story.
- Cut: the "What's next" platform pitch (*"a stepping stone toward something bigger - a full manga UGC platform"* — this is pitch-deck language, and it also ages badly if the platform never happens).
- Cut: the closing taglines (*"The machine doesn't judge. It just makes manga."* / *"And honestly? It just works."*).
- Shrink "Try it yourself" to one sentence in your old style.
- Add (if true): what *didn't* work. A hackathon project with zero admitted flaws is not believable. Even one line — a panel type it's bad at, a story genre it mangles — would do more for credibility than every superlative in the post.

| *"No signup, no complex settings, just instant gratification."* | Landing-page triple. |

**Title options:**

- "We built a manga machine over one weekend"

**Opener** (yours is close — the fix is small):

At the start of this year you could already generate manga panels with AI, but character consistency was the dealbreaker — your protagonist would look like a different person in every panel, which makes storytelling impossible.
>
> Then Google announced Gemini 2.5 Flash Image for the Nano Banana hackathon. We played with it for about five minutes in the chat interface and the character consistency seemed to actually be there. We had 48 hours, so we built something.

Some of that was us imagining what we'd want ourselves; some of it was honestly just hackathon-judging instinct.

## B. "Tambourine Voice: Open Source Dictation That Actually Gets Your Words Right" (2026-02-12)

### Overall read

This is the post where the salesy tone matters most, because of a disclosure problem the other two don't have: **it's your friend's project and you're a contributor.** You do disclose this (good — keep that exactly where it is, in paragraph one), but the disclosure makes the marketing register actively damaging. A neutral reader doing the math — friend's project + contributor + ad-formula headline + "Try it out" section + "if you write a prompt pack, share it" — files this as promotional content, and then discounts the genuinely excellent analysis in the middle.

And the middle *is* excellent. The formatter-not-assistant framing, the observation that prompt packs encode *how a profession formats its writing* rather than just vocabulary, the clipboard-swap trick — that's real insight from reading real source code. The post's best material is analytical; its packaging is promotional. Fix the packaging.

Second issue: the filename says "code-deep-dive," and the intro promises *"I went through the source code to understand why it feels so different... and I want to walk through what I found."* But what follows quotes one system-prompt line and describes features. If you keep the deep-dive framing, add actual code-reading detail (how the pipeline is structured, how the prompt layers compose, what surprised you in the implementation). Otherwise, reframe honestly as "what I learned from using it and poking at the source."

Third: the most *human* material — the music tradeoff, the spillover into using voice everywhere — is buried at the end, after the reader has already been pitched. That's your old voice trying to get out. Give it more room.

### Structural recommendation

- **Retitle** — drop the "That Actually Gets Your Words Right" formula.
- **Keep the opener's disclosure**, but cut *"I can't stop using it"* (testimonial-speak; the post itself demonstrates the point).
- **Keep the middle sections largely intact** — the formatter framing, the prompt packs, the clipboard swap. Trim the punchlines noted below.
- **Delete the "Try it out" section entirely.** The GitHub link already appears in paragraph one. Your Pokemon post proves you know how to do this in one parenthetical sentence.
- **End on the music tradeoff.** It's already written; it's the truest paragraph in the post; and ending on an unsolved personal problem instead of a pitch is *exactly* the difference between your old and new voice. "What I'd love to see next" can precede it.

## C. "The Back Button That Wasn't There" (BackToOpener draft, hidden)

### Overall read

This is the best of the three posts and the one closest to working. The story has real spine: misreading Dominic's feedback and then re-reading it correctly, the append-only history invariant killing your first design, the Safari precedent, the unglamorous Bing/MSN cleanup, and an ending that admits the experiment might fail. Those beats are all *your* old voice — honest, specific, uncertain.

The problem is a layer of performance draped over that spine. It shows up in three forms:

1. **The cinematic cold open.** Second-person, present-tense, staccato: *"You reach for the back button. / It's grey. Disabled."* This is a startup-keynote opening. Your old posts open with *you*: "I recently hosted...", "As one of the only Singaporeans...". The anecdote is real and yours — tell it as yours.

2. **Performance-review framing.** *"That alignment — browser team, search team, content team, all moving the same direction at once — felt like what platform work is supposed to feel like."* And especially: *"The distance between obvious and done is exactly where product craft lives."* These sentences are written for a promo packet or a LinkedIn repost, not for the reader of this blog. The reader can *see* the craft from the story; naming it deflates it.

3. **Engineered emphasis.** *"Real users, real behavior, real data."* *"That's non-negotiable."* *"A clean no, rather than a clever-but-risky workaround."* Each is a sentence flexing. The draft has maybe eight of these; it can afford one.

What to protect at all costs: the Dominic misread (most honest moment in all three posts), the party line (*"I'll be able to point at the back button and say: I did that. That's a pretty fun thing to be able to say at a party."* — this is charming and true and completely your voice), and *"That's what I'm writing this down for — before I know how it ends."* That last line is a genuinely great ending. The draft then dilutes it by putting the "obvious feature nobody built" thesis section *before* it — consider letting the ending carry more and the thesis section carry less.

### Structural recommendation

- **Rewrite the cold open in first person** (sample below). Everything from "A 20-year-old behavior meets a new world" onward can keep its structure.
- **Cut or fold the "The obvious feature nobody built" section.** Its factual content (coworkers' reactions, why obvious ≠ shipped) is worth one paragraph; its thesis content (*"product craft lives"*) should go entirely. Let the coworker reaction and the party line share a final section, ending on "before I know how it ends."
- **Keep the failure candor** — the misread, the rejected first design, "I'm not counting chickens." If anything, expand the misread: how long did you sit with it? Did you almost drop the proposal? That's the part a reader remembers.
- One rhythm pass: merge most one-line paragraphs into their neighbors. The em-dash count in this draft is also very high — many can become commas or parentheses (your old posts are parenthesis people, not em-dash people).

## Part 3: A voice checklist for future drafts

Run a finished draft against these before publishing:

1. **The lunch test.** Read the sentence aloud. Would you say it, verbatim, to a friend over lunch? *"The distance between obvious and done is exactly where product craft lives"* fails; *"that's a pretty fun thing to say at a party"* passes.
2. **No section whose job is selling.** If a section exists to convert the reader (Try it out, What's next as roadmap-pitch, thesis restatement), cut it. One link in one plain sentence is your ceiling — the Pokemon post's closer is the template.
3. **Keep the failure in.** Every project post gets at least one honest "this part didn't work / went unused / I'm not sure." Your old posts do this naturally; it's your credibility engine.
4. **Titles describe.** If the title contains "Actually," a superlative, or a benefit claim, rename it after what the post is about.
5. **Fragment budget: two per post.** Staccato one-liners ("It's grey. Disabled.") and "Not X. It's Y." pivots are seasoning. If the draft has five, keep the best one.
6. **Escalation needs deflation.** For every "blew my mind," there should be a "though we used it less than expected." If nothing deflates, the escalations read as copy.
7. **The reader is not a segment.** Never tell readers who they are ("If you're a doctor, a lawyer..."). Tell them what you did and what you saw; they'll decide if it's for them.
8. **Hedges are allowed.** "I think," "honestly not sure," "and I think youtiao too?" — these made the old posts feel human. Don't sand them off.
